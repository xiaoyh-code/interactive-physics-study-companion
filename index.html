Properties of Waves

Transverse vs. Longitudinal

Feature Transverse Longitudinal
Oscillation Perpendicular to travel Parallel to travel
Structure Crests & Troughs Compressions & Rarefactions
Examples EM waves, water surface Sound waves
Vacuum? No medium needed (EM) Needs material medium

Key Wave Vocabulary

Amplitude ($A$): Max displacement from the equilibrium position. [unit: $\text{m}$]

Wavelength ($\lambda$): Distance between two adjacent points in phase. [unit: $\text{m}$]

Frequency ($f$): Complete oscillations per second. [unit: $\text{Hz}$]

Period ($T$): Time taken for one complete wave. [unit: $\text{s}$]

How to Read Wave Graphs

Displacement-Time Graph

The horizontal axis is time. Read amplitude from peak heights and period ($T$) from peak-to-peak distance. Frequency is calculated via $f = 1/T$.

Displacement-Distance Graph

The horizontal axis is spatial distance. Read amplitude vertically and wavelength ($\lambda$) directly from peak-to-peak distance.

Exam Question 1 [7 Marks] Recommended: 10 mins

The displacement-time graph of a wave is observed on an oscilloscope. One complete cycle takes $5.0 \text{ ms}$. The maximum displacement from equilibrium is $3.2 \text{ cm}$.

(a) State what is meant by the amplitude of a wave. [1]

(b) State the period of this wave in seconds. [1]

(c) Calculate the frequency of the wave. [2]

(d) The wave travels at $24 \text{ m/s}$. Calculate its wavelength. [2]

(e) State whether sound is a transverse or longitudinal wave. [1]

(a) Maximum displacement from the equilibrium/rest position. [1 mark]

(b) $T = 5.0 \text{ ms} = 0.005 \text{ s}$. [1 mark]

(c) $f = 1/T = 1 / 0.005 = 200 \text{ Hz}$. [2 marks - 1 for formula, 1 for answer]

(d) $\lambda = v/f = 24 / 200 = 0.12 \text{ m}$. [2 marks - 1 for substitution, 1 for correct answer]

(e) Longitudinal wave. [1 mark]

Radio Waves

Non-ionizing

Broadcasting and long-distance cellular communications.

Common Uses:

Radio, television, communication bands.

Hazards & Protection:

None under natural conditions.

Universal Properties of EM Waves

  • All electromagnetic waves are transverse waves.
  • They do not require a medium to travel; they can travel through a complete vacuum.
  • They all travel at the speed of light in a vacuum: $c \approx 3.0 \times 10^8 \text{ m/s}$.
  • They transfer energy from point to point, and experience reflection, refraction, and diffraction.
Exam Question 3 [8 Marks] Recommended: 12 mins

(a) Arrange the following in order of increasing frequency: radio waves, ultraviolet, microwaves, gamma rays, infrared, visible light, X-rays. [3]

(b) State one medical use of X-rays. [1]

(c) Explain why bones appear lighter than soft tissue on an X-ray image. [2]

(d) A radiographer stands behind a lead screen when taking images. Explain why this is a suitable safety measure. [2]

(a) Radio, microwaves, infrared, visible light, ultraviolet, X-rays, gamma rays. [3 marks - lose 1 mark per misplaced item]

(b) Imaging of fractured bones / dental scans / cancer therapy. [1 mark]

(c) Bones are dense and absorb X-rays; soft tissue lets X-rays pass through. Thus, fewer X-rays reach the photographic film/sensor behind bones, leaving them light. [2 marks]

(d) X-rays are highly ionizing and can cause genetic mutations / cancer. Lead absorbs X-rays, preventing exposure to the health professional. [2 marks]

Chapter 12 Revision

Light Waves and Critical Angle

🔬 Lab Simulation: Critical Angle & TIR

Glass: $n = 1.5$ (Critical Angle $c \approx 41.8^\circ$)
Optically Denser Medium: Glass (Bottom)
State: Normal Refraction

Core Mathematical Formulas

Refractive Index ($n$):

n = \frac{\sin i}{\sin r}

Where $i$ is incidence in air, $r$ is refraction in dense medium.

Critical Angle Relation:

\sin c = \frac{1}{n}

Two Golden Rules for TIR

Total Internal Reflection (TIR) occurs only when BOTH criteria are met:

  1. Light must travel from an optically denser medium into an optically less dense medium (e.g., Glass to Air).
  2. The angle of incidence inside the denser medium must be greater than the critical angle ($i > c$).
Exam Question 7 [9 Marks] Recommended: 15 mins

A ray of light travels from air into a transparent glass block. Angle of incidence = $55^\circ$, Angle of refraction = $32^\circ$.

(a) State the equation linking refractive index ($n$), angle of incidence ($i$) and angle of refraction ($r$). [1]

(b) Calculate the refractive index of the block. [3]

(c) Light now travels inside the block towards the air boundary. The critical angle is $42^\circ$. State what happens when the angle of incidence inside is $30^\circ$, $42^\circ$, and $50^\circ$. [3]

(d) Explain why optical fibres use total internal reflection. [2]

(a) $n = \frac{\sin i}{\sin r}$ [1 mark]

(b) $n = \frac{\sin 55^\circ}{\sin 32^\circ} = \frac{0.8192}{0.5299} \approx 1.55$. [3 marks: formula, substitution, answer]

(c) At $30^\circ$: normal refraction (ray escapes into air, bending away from normal). At $42^\circ$: refracts at $90^\circ$ along the boundary. At $50^\circ$: Total Internal Reflection occurs ($i > c$). [3 marks]

(d) TIR ensures that light signals remain completely trapped within the core, preventing signal/energy loss over massive distances. [2 marks]

Exam Question 5 [7 Marks] Recommended: 10 mins

An oscilloscope is used to display the sound from a loudspeaker.

Time-base setting = $0.20 \text{ ms}$ per division. One complete wave occupies $2.5$ divisions. Vertical setting = $1.5 \text{ V}$ per division. The amplitude of the trace is $3$ divisions.

(a) Calculate the period of the wave. [2]

(b) Calculate the frequency of the sound. [2]

(c) Calculate the amplitude of the trace in volts. [1]

(d) The volume is increased but the pitch remains unchanged. Describe how the trace changes. [2]

(a) Period $T = 2.5 \text{ div} \times 0.20 \text{ ms/div} = 0.50 \text{ ms} = 0.0005 \text{ s}$. [2 marks]

(b) Frequency $f = 1/T = 1 / 0.0005 = 2000 \text{ Hz}$ (or $2 \text{ kHz}$). [2 marks]

(c) Amplitude $A = 3 \text{ div} \times 1.5 \text{ V/div} = 4.5 \text{ V}$. [1 mark]

(d) The vertical peak-to-peak amplitude height will increase (greater than 3 divisions). The horizontal spacing (period) remains identical. [2 marks]

Chapter 18 Revision

Density, Pressure & Hydraulics

🔬 Lab Simulator: Hydraulic Pascal Multiplication

Pascal: $F_1 / A_1 = F_2 / A_2$
Input Area ($A_1$): $0.015 \text{ m}^2$
Output Area ($A_2$): $0.450 \text{ m}^2$ (30x larger)
Output Force ($F_2$): 3000 N

Core Definitions

Density ($\rho$): Mass per unit volume. \rho = \frac{m}{V} \quad [\text{kg/m}^3]

Pressure ($p$): Force acting perpendicular to an area. p = \frac{F}{A} \quad [\text{Pa or N/m}^2]

Fluid Pressure Mechanics

Pressure deep within any resting fluid behaves via the following formula:

p = h \rho g

Where $h$ is fluid depth, $\rho$ is density, and $g$ is gravity ($10 \text{ N/kg}$). Note that fluid pressure depends only on depth and fluid density, not the container shape or surface area!

Exam Question 4 [9 Marks] Recommended: 15 mins

A submarine operates $45 \text{ m}$ below the sea surface.

Density of seawater = $1030 \text{ kg/m}^3$. Gravity $g = 10 \text{ N/kg}$. Atmospheric pressure = $1.01 \times 10^5 \text{ Pa}$.

(a) State the equation linking pressure difference, density, depth and gravitational field strength. [1]

(b) Calculate the pressure due to the seawater at this depth. [3]

(c) Calculate the total pressure acting on the submarine. [2]

(d) The submarine dives deeper. Explain why pressure increases with depth. [2]

(e) State whether pressure depends on the surface area of the submarine. [1]

(a) $p = h \rho g$ [1 mark]

(b) $p = 45 \times 1030 \times 10 = 463,500 \text{ Pa}$ (or $4.635 \times 10^5 \text{ Pa}$). [3 marks]

(c) $p_{\text{total}} = p_{\text{seawater}} + p_{\text{atm}} = 4.635 \times 10^5 + 1.01 \times 10^5 = 5.645 \times 10^5 \text{ Pa}$. [2 marks]

(d) As depth increases, the weight of the water column above the submarine increases. Since pressure is force over area, this greater weight increases the pressure. [2 marks]

(e) No. Fluid pressure depends only on depth and fluid density. [1 mark]

Chapter 19 Revision

Thermal Physics & Gas Laws

🔬 Lab Simulator: Heating Curve & Particles

Specific vs. Latent Heat

Click on any thermal zone of the heating graph to inspect particle arrangement and heat energy equations.

Ice Phase (Solid)

Q = mc\Delta T

Solid particles are bound tightly in a regular, close lattice. They vibrate on spot. Adding heat energy causes kinetic energy and temperature to rise.

Core Thermodynamic Calculations

Specific Heat Capacity ($c$): Energy to raise 1 kg by 1°C. Q = mc\Delta T

Specific Latent Heat ($L$): Energy to change state of 1 kg with ZERO temperature change. Q = mL

Ideal Gas Laws

Condition Relationship Formula
Constant Temp $p \propto 1/V$ $p_1 V_1 = p_2 V_2$
Constant Vol $p \propto T$ $p_1 / T_1 = p_2 / T_2$
Constant Press $V \propto T$ $V_1 / T_1 = V_2 / T_2$

🚨 CRITICAL RULE: Always convert Celsius temperatures to Kelvin: $T(\text{K}) = \theta(^\circ\text{C}) + 273$ before calculating!

Exam Question 6 [10 Marks] Recommended: 15 mins

A student uses steam at $100^\circ\text{C}$ to heat $0.40 \text{ kg}$ of milk from $12^\circ\text{C}$ to $65^\circ\text{C}$.

Specific heat capacity of milk = $3900 \text{ J / kg}^\circ\text{C}$. Latent heat of vaporisation of water = $2.3 \times 10^6 \text{ J/kg}$.

(a) Calculate the energy needed to heat the milk to $65^\circ\text{C}$. [3]

(b) Calculate the mass of steam needed if only condensation latent heat is considered. [3]

(c) In reality, the condensed water cools from $100^\circ\text{C}$ to $65^\circ\text{C}$. Explain whether the actual steam mass needed is smaller or larger than (b). [2]

(a) $Q = mc\Delta T = 0.40 \times 3900 \times (65 - 12) = 0.40 \times 3900 \times 53 = 82,680 \text{ J}$. [3 marks]

(b) $Q = mL \implies m = Q/L = 82,680 / (2.3 \times 10^6) \approx 0.036 \text{ kg}$ (or $36 \text{ g}$). [3 marks]

(c) Smaller. The water cooled from $100^\circ\text{C}$ to $65^\circ\text{C}$ will also release sensible heat ($Q = mc\Delta T$), meaning less overall steam mass is required to meet the energy quota. [2 marks]

Q12 - Integrated Mechanical & Thermal Challenge [10 Marks]

Hard

A metal container has a base area of $0.080 \text{ m}^2$. It contains $6.0 \text{ kg}$ of water. The water is heated from $18^\circ\text{C}$ to $100^\circ\text{C}$.
Given: $c_{\text{water}} = 4200 \text{ J / kg}^\circ\text{C}$, $g = 10 \text{ N/kg}$, $\rho_{\text{water}} = 1000 \text{ kg/m}^3$.

(a) Calculate the thermal energy needed to heat the water to $100^\circ\text{C}$. [3]

(b) Calculate the weight of the water. [1]

(c) Calculate the pressure exerted by the water on the container base due to its weight. [2]

(d) Use density to calculate the volume of water. [2]

(e) Calculate the depth of water in the container. [1]

(f) Verify your pressure answer using $p = h \rho g$. [1]

(a) $Q = mc\Delta T = 6.0 \times 4200 \times (100 - 18) = 2,066,400 \text{ J}$. [3 marks]

(b) $W = mg = 6.0 \times 10 = 60 \text{ N}$. [1 mark]

(c) $p = F/A = 60 \text{ N} / 0.080 \text{ m}^2 = 750 \text{ Pa}$. [2 marks]

(d) $V = m/\rho = 6.0 / 1000 = 0.006 \text{ m}^3$ (or $6.0 \text{ L}$). [2 marks]

(e) $V = A \times h \implies h = V/A = 0.006 / 0.080 = 0.075 \text{ m}$ ($7.5 \text{ cm}$). [1 mark]

(f) $p = h \rho g = 0.075 \times 1000 \times 10 = 750 \text{ Pa}$. (Matches (c) perfectly!) [1 mark]
