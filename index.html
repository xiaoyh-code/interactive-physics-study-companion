Key Wave Vocabulary

Amplitude ($A$): Max displacement from the equilibrium position. [unit: $\text{m}$]

Wavelength ($\lambda$): Distance between two adjacent points in phase. [unit: $\text{m}$]

Frequency ($f$): Complete oscillations per second. [unit: $\text{Hz}$]

Period ($T$): Time taken for one complete wave. [unit: $\text{s}$]

How to Read Wave Graphs

Displacement-Time Graph

The horizontal axis is time. Read amplitude from peak heights and period ($T$) from peak-to-peak distance. Frequency is calculated via $f = 1/T$.

Displacement-Distance Graph

The horizontal axis is spatial distance. Read amplitude vertically and wavelength ($\lambda$) directly from peak-to-peak distance.

Exam Question 1 [7 Marks] Recommended: 10 mins

The displacement-time graph of a wave is observed on an oscilloscope. One complete cycle takes $5.0 \text{ ms}$. The maximum displacement from equilibrium is $3.2 \text{ cm}$.

(a) State what is meant by the amplitude of a wave. [1]

(b) State the period of this wave in seconds. [1]

(c) Calculate the frequency of the wave. [2]

(d) The wave travels at $24 \text{ m/s}$. Calculate its wavelength. [2]

(e) State whether sound is a transverse or longitudinal wave. [1]

(a) Maximum displacement from the equilibrium/rest position. [1 mark]

(b) $T = 5.0 \text{ ms} = 0.005 \text{ s}$. [1 mark]

(c) $f = 1/T = 1 / 0.005 = 200 \text{ Hz}$. [2 marks - 1 for formula, 1 for answer]

(d) $\lambda = v/f = 24 / 200 = 0.12 \text{ m}$. [2 marks - 1 for substitution, 1 for correct answer]

(e) Longitudinal wave. [1 mark]

Radio Waves

Non-ionizing

Broadcasting and long-distance cellular communications.

Common Uses:

Radio, television, communication bands.

Hazards & Protection:

None under natural conditions.

Universal Properties of EM Waves

  • All electromagnetic waves are transverse waves.
  • They do not require a medium to travel; they can travel through a complete vacuum.
  • They all travel at the speed of light in a vacuum: $c \approx 3.0 \times 10^8 \text{ m/s}$.
  • They transfer energy from point to point, and experience reflection, refraction, and diffraction.

Chapter 12 Revision

Light Waves and Critical Angle

🔬 Lab Simulation: Critical Angle & TIR

Glass: $n = 1.5$ (Critical Angle $c \approx 41.8^\circ$)
Optically Denser Medium: Glass (Bottom)
State: Normal Refraction

Core Mathematical Formulas

Refractive Index ($n$):

n = \frac{\sin i}{\sin r}

Where $i$ is incidence in air, $r$ is refraction in dense medium.

Critical Angle Relation:

\sin c = \frac{1}{n}

Two Golden Rules for TIR

Total Internal Reflection (TIR) occurs only when BOTH criteria are met:

  1. Light must travel from an optically denser medium into an optically less dense medium (e.g., Glass to Air).
  2. The angle of incidence inside the denser medium must be greater than the critical angle ($i > c$).
Exam Question 7 [9 Marks] Recommended: 15 mins

A ray of light travels from air into a transparent glass block. Angle of incidence = $55^\circ$, Angle of refraction = $32^\circ$.

(a) State the equation linking refractive index ($n$), angle of incidence ($i$) and angle of refraction ($r$). [1]

(b) Calculate the refractive index of the block. [3]

(c) Light now travels inside the block towards the air boundary. The critical angle is $42^\circ$. State what happens when the angle of incidence inside is $30^\circ$, $42^\circ$, and $50^\circ$. [3]

(d) Explain why optical fibres use total internal reflection. [2]

(a) $n = \frac{\sin i}{\sin r}$ [1 mark]

(b) $n = \frac{\sin 55^\circ}{\sin 32^\circ} = \frac{0.8192}{0.5299} \approx 1.55$. [3 marks: formula, substitution, answer]

(c) At $30^\circ$: normal refraction (ray escapes into air, bending away from normal). At $42^\circ$: refracts at $90^\circ$ along the boundary. At $50^\circ$: Total Internal Reflection occurs ($i > c$). [3 marks]

(d) TIR ensures that light signals remain completely trapped within the core, preventing signal/energy loss over massive distances. [2 marks]

An oscilloscope is used to display the sound from a loudspeaker.

Chapter 18 Revision

Density, Pressure & Hydraulics

🔬 Lab Simulator: Hydraulic Pascal Multiplication

Pascal: $F_1 / A_1 = F_2 / A_2$
Input Area ($A_1$): $0.015 \text{ m}^2$
Output Area ($A_2$): $0.450 \text{ m}^2$ (30x larger)
Output Force ($F_2$): 3000 N

Core Definitions

Density ($\rho$): Mass per unit volume. \rho = \frac{m}{V} \quad [\text{kg/m}^3]

Pressure ($p$): Force acting perpendicular to an area. p = \frac{F}{A} \quad [\text{Pa or N/m}^2]

Fluid Pressure Mechanics

Pressure deep within any resting fluid behaves via the following formula:

p = h \rho g

Where $h$ is fluid depth, $\rho$ is density, and $g$ is gravity ($10 \text{ N/kg}$). Note that fluid pressure depends only on depth and fluid density, not the container shape or surface area!

Exam Question 4 [9 Marks] Recommended: 15 mins

A submarine operates $45 \text{ m}$ below the sea surface.

Density of seawater = $1030 \text{ kg/m}^3$. Gravity $g = 10 \text{ N/kg}$. Atmospheric pressure = $1.01 \times 10^5 \text{ Pa}$.

(a) State the equation linking pressure difference, density, depth and gravitational field strength. [1]

(b) Calculate the pressure due to the seawater at this depth. [3]

(c) Calculate the total pressure acting on the submarine. [2]

(d) The submarine dives deeper. Explain why pressure increases with depth. [2]

(e) State whether pressure depends on the surface area of the submarine. [1]

(a) $p = h \rho g$ [1 mark]

(b) $p = 45 \times 1030 \times 10 = 463,500 \text{ Pa}$ (or $4.635 \times 10^5 \text{ Pa}$). [3 marks]

(c) $p_{\text{total}} = p_{\text{seawater}} + p_{\text{atm}} = 4.635 \times 10^5 + 1.01 \times 10^5 = 5.645 \times 10^5 \text{ Pa}$. [2 marks]

(d) As depth increases, the weight of the water column above the submarine increases. Since pressure is force over area, this greater weight increases the pressure. [2 marks]

(e) No. Fluid pressure depends only on depth and fluid density. [1 mark]

Chapter 19 Revision

Thermal Physics & Gas Laws

🔬 Lab Simulator: Heating Curve & Particles

Specific vs. Latent Heat

Click on any thermal zone of the heating graph to inspect particle arrangement and heat energy equations.

Ice Phase (Solid)

Q = mc\Delta T

Solid particles are bound tightly in a regular, close lattice. They vibrate on spot. Adding heat energy causes kinetic energy and temperature to rise.

Core Thermodynamic Calculations

Specific Heat Capacity ($c$): Energy to raise 1 kg by 1°C. Q = mc\Delta T

Specific Latent Heat ($L$): Energy to change state of 1 kg with ZERO temperature change. Q = mL

Ideal Gas Laws

Condition Relationship Formula
Constant Temp $p \propto 1/V$ $p_1 V_1 = p_2 V_2$
Constant Vol $p \propto T$ $p_1 / T_1 = p_2 / T_2$
Constant Press $V \propto T$ $V_1 / T_1 = V_2 / T_2$

🚨 CRITICAL RULE: Always convert Celsius temperatures to Kelvin: $T(\text{K}) = \theta(^\circ\text{C}) + 273$ before calculating!

Exam Question 6 [10 Marks] Recommended: 15 mins

A student uses steam at $100^\circ\text{C}$ to heat $0.40 \text{ kg}$ of milk from $12^\circ\text{C}$ to $65^\circ\text{C}$.

Specific heat capacity of milk = $3900 \text{ J / kg}^\circ\text{C}$. Latent heat of vaporisation of water = $2.3 \times 10^6 \text{ J/kg}$.

(a) Calculate the energy needed to heat the milk to $65^\circ\text{C}$. [3]

(b) Calculate the mass of steam needed if only condensation latent heat is considered. [3]

(c) In reality, the condensed water cools from $100^\circ\text{C}$ to $65^\circ\text{C}$. Explain whether the actual steam mass needed is smaller or larger than (b). [2]

(a) $Q = mc\Delta T = 0.40 \times 3900 \times (65 - 12) = 0.40 \times 3900 \times 53 = 82,680 \text{ J}$. [3 marks]

(b) $Q = mL \implies m = Q/L = 82,680 / (2.3 \times 10^6) \approx 0.036 \text{ kg}$ (or $36 \text{ g}$). [3 marks]

(c) Smaller. The water cooled from $100^\circ\text{C}$ to $65^\circ\text{C}$ will also release sensible heat ($Q = mc\Delta T$), meaning less overall steam mass is required to meet the energy quota. [2 marks]

A. Density and Pressure

Worked Pattern: Density by Displacement

[Practice]

A stone is lowered into a measuring cylinder.

Initial water volume $= 42\,\text{cm}^3$. Final volume $= 68\,\text{cm}^3$. Mass of stone $= 78\,\text{g}$.

$$V = 68 - 42 = 26\,\text{cm}^3$$

Mini Practice: Hydraulic Force

[3 marks]

A force of $60\,\text{N}$ acts on a small piston of area $0.0020\,\text{m}^2$. The output piston has area $0.050\,\text{m}^2$. Calculate the output force.

$$p = \frac{F_1}{A_1} = \frac{60}{0.0020} = 30\,000\,\text{Pa}$$

$$F_2 = pA_2 = 30\,000 \times 0.050 = 1500\,\text{N}$$

Q4 — Fluid Pressure and Total Pressure

[9 marks]

A submarine is $45\,\text{m}$ below the surface of the sea.

Density of seawater $= 1030\,\text{kg m}^{-3}$. Gravitational field strength $= 10\,\text{N kg}^{-1}$. Atmospheric pressure $= 1.01 \times 10^5\,\text{Pa}$.

  2. (b) $$\Delta p = 1030 \times 10 \times 45 = 4.64 \times 10^5\,\text{Pa}$$
  3. (c) $$p_{\text{total}} = 4.64 \times 10^5 + 1.01 \times 10^5 = 5.65 \times 10^5\,\text{Pa}$$
  4. (d) Pressure increases because the weight of the fluid above the submarine increases with depth.
  5. (e) No; pressure at a given depth does not depend on the surface area of the submarine.

[7 marks]

B. States of Matter

Mini Practice: Evaporative Cooling

[3 marks]

Explain why wet skin feels cooler when air blows across it. Use ideas about evaporation and molecular kinetic energy.

Air movement increases the rate of evaporation. The fastest molecules escape from the water surface, so the average kinetic energy of the remaining molecules decreases. Since temperature depends on average kinetic energy, the liquid (and skin) cools.

C. Specific Heat Capacity and Latent Heat

Worked Pattern: Specific Heat Capacity

[Practice]

A $0.80\,\text{kg}$ metal block is heated by a $75\,\text{W}$ heater for $6.0\,\text{min}$. Its temperature rises from $19^\circ\text{C}$ to $44^\circ\text{C}$.

$$E = Pt = 75 \times (6.0 \times 60) = 27\,000\,\text{J}$$

$$\Delta T = 44 - 19 = 25^\circ\text{C}$$

$$c = \frac{E}{m\Delta T} = \frac{27\,000}{0.80 \times 25} = 1350\,\text{J kg}^{-1}\text{K}^{-1}$$

Mini Practice: Specific Latent Heat

[3 marks]

A heater supplies $40\,\text{W}$ for $240\,\text{s}$. With the heater on, $58\,\text{g}$ of liquid is collected. With the heater off, $10\,\text{g}$ is collected. Calculate the specific latent heat.

$$m_{\text{heater only}} = 58 - 10 = 48\,\text{g} = 0.048\,\text{kg}$$

$$E = Pt = 40 \times 240 = 9600\,\text{J}$$

$$L = \frac{Pt}{m_{\text{heater only}}} = \frac{9600}{0.048} = 2.0 \times 10^5\,\text{J kg}^{-1}$$

Q6 — Thermal Energy and Latent Heat

[10 marks]

A student uses steam at $100^\circ\text{C}$ to heat $0.40\,\text{kg}$ of milk from $12^\circ\text{C}$ to $65^\circ\text{C}$.

Specific heat capacity of milk $= 3900\,\text{J kg}^{-1}\,^\circ\text{C}^{-1}$. Specific latent heat of vaporisation of water $= 2.3 \times 10^6\,\text{J kg}^{-1}$. Specific heat capacity of water $= 4200\,\text{J kg}^{-1}\,^\circ\text{C}^{-1}$.

Assume that all the energy released by the steam is transferred to the milk.

  1. (a) Calculate the energy needed to heat the milk to $65^\circ\text{C}$. [3]
  2. (b) When steam condenses to water at $100^\circ\text{C}$, it releases latent heat. Calculate the mass of steam needed if only condensation is considered. [3]
  3. (c) In reality, the condensed water cools from $100^\circ\text{C}$ to $65^\circ\text{C}$. Explain whether the actual mass of steam needed is smaller or larger than your answer in part (b). [2]
  4. (d) Explain why temperature does not change while steam is condensing. [2]
  1. (a) $$Q = mc\Delta T = 0.40 \times 3900 \times (65 - 12) = 8.27 \times 10^4\,\text{J}$$
  2. (b) $$m = \frac{Q}{L} = \frac{8.27 \times 10^4}{2.3 \times 10^6} = 0.036\,\text{kg}\,(36\,\text{g})$$
  3. (c) Smaller. The condensed water also releases heat as it cools from $100^\circ\text{C}$ to $65^\circ\text{C}$, so less steam is needed to provide the same energy.
  4. (d) During condensation, energy is used to overcome intermolecular forces / break bonds, not to increase the average kinetic energy of the molecules, so the temperature stays constant.

Q12 — Integrated Thermal and Pressure Challenge

[10 marks]

A metal container has a base area of $0.080\,\text{m}^2$. It contains $6.0\,\text{kg}$ of water. The water is heated from $18^\circ\text{C}$ to $100^\circ\text{C}$.

Specific heat capacity of water $= 4200\,\text{J kg}^{-1}\,^\circ\text{C}^{-1}$. Gravitational field strength $= 10\,\text{N kg}^{-1}$. Density of water $= 1000\,\text{kg m}^{-3}$.

  1. (a) Calculate the thermal energy needed to heat the water to $100^\circ\text{C}$. [3]
  2. (b) Calculate the weight of the water. [1]
  3. (c) Calculate the pressure exerted by the water on the base of the container due to its weight. [2]
  4. (d) Use density to calculate the volume of water in the container. [2]
  5. (e) Calculate the depth of water in the container. [1]
  6. (f) Check your answer to part (c) by using $p = h\rho g$. [1]
  1. (a) $$Q = mc\Delta T = 6.0 \times 4200 \times (100 - 18) = 2.07 \times 10^6\,\text{J}$$
  2. (b) $$W = mg = 6.0 \times 10 = 60\,\text{N}$$
  3. (c) $$p = \frac{F}{A} = \frac{60}{0.080} = 750\,\text{Pa}$$
  4. (d) $$V = \frac{m}{\rho} = \frac{6.0}{1000} = 6.0 \times 10^{-3}\,\text{m}^3$$
  5. (e) $$h = \frac{V}{A} = \frac{6.0 \times 10^{-3}}{0.080} = 0.075\,\text{m}$$
  6. (f) $$p = h\rho g = 0.075 \times 1000 \times 10 = 750\,\text{Pa}$$

D. Gas Laws and Kinetic Theory

Worked Pattern: Charles' Law

[Practice]

A gas has volume $1.20 \times 10^{-3}\,\text{m}^3$ at $18^\circ\text{C}$. It is warmed to $63^\circ\text{C}$ at constant pressure.

$$T_1 = 18 + 273 = 291\,\text{K},\quad T_2 = 63 + 273 = 336\,\text{K}$$

$$V_2 = \frac{V_1T_2}{T_1} = \frac{(1.20 \times 10^{-3}) \times 336}{291} = 1.39 \times 10^{-3}\,\text{m}^3$$

Mini Practice: Mean Kinetic Energy

[3 marks]

Calculate the mean kinetic energy of one molecule at $320\,\text{K}$. Then explain what happens to the mean kinetic energy if the gas is compressed at constant temperature.

$$\frac{1}{2}m c_{\text{rms}}^2 = \frac{3}{2}kT = 1.5 \times 1.38 \times 10^{-23} \times 320 = 6.6 \times 10^{-21}\,\text{J}$$

If the gas is compressed at constant temperature, the mean kinetic energy stays the same because mean kinetic energy is directly proportional to absolute temperature.

Q2 — Gas Laws

[9 marks]

A sealed syringe contains $80\,\text{cm}^3$ of air at a pressure of $1.05 \times 10^5\,\text{Pa}$ and a temperature of $27^\circ\text{C}$.

The plunger is pushed in so that the volume becomes $50\,\text{cm}^3$. The temperature of the air then rises to $47^\circ\text{C}$.

  1. (a) Convert both temperatures to kelvin. [2]
  2. (b) State the gas law relationship that should be used when pressure, volume and temperature all change for a fixed mass of gas. [1]
  3. (c) Calculate the final pressure of the air. [4]
  4. (d) Explain, using ideas about molecules, why reducing the volume increases the gas pressure. [2]
  1. (a) $T_1 = 27 + 273 = 300\,\text{K}$; $T_2 = 47 + 273 = 320\,\text{K}$
  2. (b) $$\frac{p_1V_1}{T_1} = \frac{p_2V_2}{T_2}$$
  3. (c) $$p_2 = \frac{p_1V_1T_2}{V_2T_1} = \frac{1.05 \times 10^5 \times 80 \times 320}{50 \times 300} = 1.79 \times 10^5\,\text{Pa}$$
  4. (d) Reducing the volume makes molecules travel a shorter distance between collisions with the walls, so collisions are more frequent, producing a greater force per unit area and therefore higher pressure.

Q8 — Ideal Gas Law from Data

[8 marks]

A sealed glass bulb contains helium gas.

Volume of bulb $= 2.4 \times 10^{-3}\,\text{m}^3$. Pressure $= 1.20 \times 10^5\,\text{Pa}$. Temperature $= 22^\circ\text{C}$. Boltzmann constant $= 1.38 \times 10^{-23}\,\text{J K}^{-1}$.

  1. (a) State the ideal gas equation using number of molecules $N$. [1]
  2. (b) Convert the temperature to kelvin. [1]
  3. (c) Calculate the number of helium atoms in the bulb. [4]
  4. (d) The temperature is increased while the volume remains constant. Explain what happens to the pressure in terms of molecular motion. [2]
  1. (a) $$pV = NkT$$
  2. (b) $T = 22 + 273 = 295\,\text{K}$
  3. (c) $$N = \frac{pV}{kT} = \frac{1.20 \times 10^5 \times 2.4 \times 10^{-3}}{1.38 \times 10^{-23} \times 295} = 7.1 \times 10^{22}$$
  4. (d) Molecules move faster / have greater average kinetic energy; they collide with the walls more often and with greater force, so pressure increases.

Q11 — Kinetic Theory of Gases

[9 marks]

The mass of one molecule of a gas is $4.8 \times 10^{-26}\,\text{kg}$. The gas is at a temperature of $320\,\text{K}$. Boltzmann constant $= 1.38 \times 10^{-23}\,\text{J K}^{-1}$.

The kinetic theory equation is $\frac{1}{2}m c_{\text{rms}}^2 = \frac{3}{2}kT$.

  1. (a) State what is meant by root-mean-square speed. [1]
  2. (b) Rearrange the equation to make $c_{\text{rms}}$ the subject. [2]
  3. (c) Calculate the root-mean-square speed of the gas molecules. [3]
  4. (d) The gas is heated at constant volume. Explain why the pressure increases. Your answer should refer to molecular speed, collisions and force on the container wall. [3]
  1. (a) The square root of the mean of the squares of the speeds of the molecules.
  2. (b) $$c_{\text{rms}} = \sqrt{\frac{3kT}{m}}$$
  3. (c) $$c_{\text{rms}} = \sqrt{\frac{3 \times 1.38 \times 10^{-23} \times 320}{4.8 \times 10^{-26}}} = 5.3 \times 10^2\,\text{m s}^{-1}$$
  4. (d) Heating increases the average kinetic energy, so molecules move faster. They hit the walls more often and with greater force. Since pressure is force per unit area, the pressure on the container walls increases.

Topic-Paper Practice: Part II

A. Density and Pressure

A1 — Density in Two Units

[4 marks]

A $144\,\text{g}$ object raises the water level in a cylinder from $38\,\text{cm}^3$ to $62\,\text{cm}^3$. Calculate its density in $\text{g cm}^{-3}$ and $\text{kg m}^{-3}$.

$$V = 62 - 38 = 24\,\text{cm}^3$$

$$\rho = \frac{m}{V} = \frac{144}{24} = 6.0\,\text{g cm}^{-3} = 6000\,\text{kg m}^{-3}$$

A2 — Pressure from Weight

[2 marks]

A box has weight $480\,\text{N}$ and base area $0.32\,\text{m}^2$. Calculate the pressure on the floor.

$$p = \frac{F}{A} = \frac{480}{0.32} = 1500\,\text{Pa}$$

A3 — Pressure at Depth

[3 marks]

Calculate the pressure due to oil of density $850\,\text{kg m}^{-3}$ at a depth of $4.5\,\text{m}$.

$$\Delta p = \rho gh = 850 \times 10 \times 4.5 = 3.83 \times 10^4\,\text{Pa}$$

B. Thermal Physics

B1 — Specific Heat Capacity

[3 marks]

A $1.5\,\text{kg}$ liquid is heated by $63\,000\,\text{J}$ and its temperature rises by $20\,\text{K}$. Calculate its specific heat capacity.

$$c = \frac{Q}{m\Delta T} = \frac{63\,000}{1.5 \times 20} = 2100\,\text{J kg}^{-1}\text{K}^{-1}$$

B2 — Latent Heat of Fusion

[2 marks]

$0.20\,\text{kg}$ of a substance melts at constant temperature. Its specific latent heat of fusion is $1.8 \times 10^5\,\text{J kg}^{-1}$. Calculate the energy needed.

$$Q = mL = 0.20 \times 1.8 \times 10^5 = 3.6 \times 10^4\,\text{J}$$

B3 — Constant Temperature During Melting

[2 marks]

Explain why temperature remains constant during melting even though energy is supplied.

Energy is used to overcome intermolecular forces and break the rigid structure, not to increase the average kinetic energy of the particles. Since temperature depends on average kinetic energy, it remains constant.

C. Gas Laws and Kinetic Theory

C1 — Boyle's Law

[2 marks]

A gas has pressure $120\,\text{kPa}$ at volume $75\,\text{cm}^3$. It is compressed to $45\,\text{cm}^3$ at constant temperature. Calculate the new pressure.

$$p_2 = \frac{p_1V_1}{V_2} = \frac{120 \times 75}{45} = 200\,\text{kPa}$$

C2 — Pressure Law

[3 marks]

A sealed gas has pressure $95\,\text{kPa}$ at $20^\circ\text{C}$. It is heated to $80^\circ\text{C}$ at constant volume. Calculate the new pressure.

$$T_1 = 293\,\text{K},\quad T_2 = 353\,\text{K}$$

$$p_2 = \frac{p_1T_2}{T_1} = \frac{95 \times 353}{293} = 114\,\text{kPa}$$

C3 — Pressure Rise on Heating

[3 marks]

Use kinetic theory to explain why pressure rises when a sealed gas is heated.

When heated, molecules have greater average kinetic energy and move faster. They collide with the walls more often and with greater force. Since pressure is force per unit area, the pressure increases.
